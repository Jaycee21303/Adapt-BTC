Treasury strategy

Why businesses are adding Bitcoin to diversify reserves

Adding a measured Bitcoin allocation gives companies exposure to a scarce, global asset that isn’t tied to one banking system. It can complement cash and short-term treasuries while improving liquidity options across borders.

  • Reduce single-currency risk by holding a digitally scarce reserve that cannot be diluted.
  • Keep optionality: cold storage for long-term runway plus Lightning or exchange liquidity for operational needs.
  • Documented policies—allocation bands, signers, and rebalancing rules—help boards and finance teams stay aligned.

Two complementary roles

Bitcoin works as both a long-term asset and everyday payments rail

Some people view Bitcoin primarily as a scarce savings asset—digital property that protects wealth over decades. Others focus on its utility as fast, final settlement for day-to-day payments, especially over the Lightning Network. Both viewpoints are correct, and they reinforce each other.

  • Asset lens: The 21M cap and global liquidity make Bitcoin a modern reserve asset that resists debasement and travels across borders in minutes.
  • Payments lens: Instant, low-fee transfers let shoppers and vendors avoid card fees and chargebacks while settling directly.
  • Why both matter: Savings demand strengthens network security, while real-world payments prove usefulness and keep liquidity flowing.

How to balance both

For individuals

Hold long-term savings in cold storage while keeping a small Lightning balance for daily purchases or peer-to-peer transfers.

For businesses

Maintain a treasury allocation for diversification, plus a controlled operating float for Lightning payments and instant settlement.

Bottom line

Bitcoin’s strength comes from being both durable savings and spendable money—adopt it in the way that fits your time horizon.

Mainstream access

Bitcoin ETFs make exposure easier—know the trade-offs

Spot Bitcoin ETFs now trade on major U.S. exchanges. They offer familiar brokerage access but have fees and only move during market hours. Holding your own keys remains the gold standard for censorship-resistance.

Popular issuers

  • iShares Bitcoin Trust (BlackRock)
  • Fidelity Wise Origin
  • VanEck Bitcoin Trust

What to keep in mind

  • Expense ratios, brokerage commissions, and taxable events apply.
  • Trading halts and market hours can limit exits during volatility.
  • ETF shares ≠ self-custodied Bitcoin—you can’t spend or withdraw them.

How to use them wisely

  • Try-before-self-custody: ETFs can be a first step for newcomers who already have a brokerage account; plan a migration to holding keys when comfortable.
  • Keep fees in check: Compare annual expense ratios (many range from 0.19–0.39%) and watch bid/ask spreads on low-volume tickers.
  • Stay in control: For censorship resistance and global spending, move to a wallet you control. Our beginner guide walks through the transition.

Why fixed supply matters

Inflation isn’t abstract — it’s trillions of new dollars

Since 2020, the U.S. expanded its money supply by trillions of dollars to fund stimulus and bailouts. That dilution shows up as higher grocery bills, rising rent, and shrinking savings rates. Bitcoin’s 21 million cap is a counterweight: issuance is pre-programmed, visible, and cannot be changed by a vote or emergency meeting.

$5T+

Pandemic-era printing swelled the U.S. monetary base in 2020–2021, a shock that fueled 40-year-high CPI readings.

Bank runs

Regional bank failures in 2023 showed how quickly withdrawal limits and weekend closures appear; self-custodied BTC remained accessible 24/7.

Hard cap

Bitcoin enforces scarcity with a predictable halving schedule—no surprise issuance, no dilution when governments overspend.

Spot the difference

  • Fiat playbook: Central banks expand balance sheets in crises, often adding hundreds of billions in weeks.
  • Bitcoin playbook: Supply growth halves every ~210,000 blocks; anyone can audit issuance in real time.
  • What it means for you: Savings held in cash or low-yield accounts get quietly diluted—sats in cold storage do not.
