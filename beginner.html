What Is Bitcoin?

Bitcoin is digital, peer-to-peer money that runs on a decentralized network of computers called nodes. No single country, bank, or company controls it, and transactions are recorded transparently on a public ledger (the blockchain).

Neutral

Anyone can use Bitcoin without permission—there are no gatekeepers.

Scarce

Only 21 million will ever exist, making it resistant to inflation.

Portable

Move value globally in minutes without banking hours or borders.

Think of Bitcoin as an open monetary network—similar to how email is an open communication network. You can save, send, and receive funds directly, and the rules are enforced by code rather than by institutions.

Why Bitcoin Matters (For Beginners)

Scarcity

Only 21 million Bitcoin will ever exist. This fixed supply makes it more resistant to inflation and forced dilution of your savings.

Ownership

You hold Bitcoin yourself — no bank can freeze it, block transfers, or change your balance.

Borderless

You can send Bitcoin worldwide in minutes with no middlemen or restrictions.

Secure Network

Bitcoin is protected by the largest decentralized computing network ever created.

Predictable Monetary Policy

Bitcoin’s issuance schedule is transparent, fixed, and cannot be changed by anyone.

Global Adoption

Millions of users, companies, and institutions now hold Bitcoin as savings or treasury assets.

Why the fixed 21M supply matters

inflation + bank run resilience

Inflation protection

Central banks can expand money supply quickly (e.g., 2008-2009 quantitative easing, 2020 stimulus), which erodes purchasing power.

Bitcoin’s code enforces the 21 million cap, so new coins are issued on a predictable schedule and cannot be printed to bail out debts.

Concrete examples

  • 1970s–1980s: USD inflation topped 10%+ annually; savers lost buying power.
  • 2008–2012: Monetary base more than doubled; housing crash bailouts diluted cash savings.
  • 2020–2022: Rapid stimulus raised prices for groceries, rent, and energy worldwide.

Bank run resilience

When too many people withdraw at once (e.g., 1930s bank runs, 2008 Lehman collapse, 2023 regional bank failures), banks can freeze funds.

Holding your own keys means you are not relying on a bank’s liquidity or withdrawal limits; you can move value anytime, anywhere.

Bottom line for beginners

Bitcoin combines a fixed supply with self-custody. You can opt out of surprise money printing and avoid being locked out of your funds during a crisis by learning to control your own wallet.

Beginner Starter Checklist

5-minute setup
1

Pick a wallet type

Hardware wallets (Ledger, Trezor) give the strongest protection; mobile wallets (Muun, Phoenix) are easy on the go.

2

Back up your seed phrase

Write the 12–24 words on paper (or metal) and store it in two separate safe places.

3

Buy small amounts

Use reputable exchanges, then withdraw to your wallet to learn the process.

4

Test sending & receiving

Send a tiny amount to yourself to build confidence before larger deposits.

5

Keep learning

Understand fees, on-chain vs Lightning, and practice safe backups before investing more.

6

Enable protections

Add a PIN to your wallet app, enable 2FA on exchanges, and lock down your email with a strong password manager.

How Do I Get Started?

  1. Choose a Bitcoin Wallet — A safe place to hold your Bitcoin. Hardware wallets are best.
  2. Buy Bitcoin — Use trusted exchanges like Coinbase, Kraken, or Cash App.
  3. Withdraw to Your Wallet — Don’t leave Bitcoin on exchanges long-term.
  4. Learn About Security — Seed phrases, backups, hardware wallets, and avoiding scams.
  5. Start Small — Dollar-cost average (DCA) into Bitcoin slowly over time.

Bitcoin ETFs: convenient exposure, different trade-offs

spot ETFs explained

Spot Bitcoin ETFs let you buy price exposure through a brokerage account. They are easier for retirement accounts and compliance, but they are not the same as holding Bitcoin yourself and they charge annual fees.

Notable U.S. tickers

  • IBIT (BlackRock iShares Bitcoin Trust)
  • FBTC (Fidelity Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund)
  • HODL (VanEck Bitcoin Trust)

Check your broker’s availability and expense ratio.

Pros

  • Use existing brokerage or retirement accounts.
  • 1099 tax documents simplify reporting in the U.S.
  • No private key management required to start.

Cons

  • Expense ratios (often 0.19–0.39%) reduce returns.
  • Trades only during market hours; can’t send or spend.
  • Issuer holds the Bitcoin—no self-custody or Lightning.

When to consider an ETF vs. buying Bitcoin directly

ETFs can be a bridge if you’re limited to brokerage accounts, need simplified tax forms, or want to ease in before learning self-custody. If you want permissionless payments, global access, or to minimize custodial risk, learn to withdraw to your own wallet. We can help you design a transition plan.

Security essentials for new Bitcoiners

protect your keys

Backups

  • Write down seed phrase offline—never store in email or photos.
  • Keep two copies in separate, private locations.
  • Practice restoring with a small amount before trusting large sums.

Avoid scams

  • No support rep will ever ask for your seed phrase.
  • Bookmark official sites; double-check download links.
  • Use a hardware wallet for long-term holdings.

Online hygiene

  • Use a password manager and enable 2FA (authenticator app, not SMS).
  • Lock your phone and computer with strong passcodes.
  • Avoid public Wi‑Fi for wallet activity when possible.

Quick FAQ

answers in plain English
Do I need to buy a whole Bitcoin?
No. Bitcoin is divisible into 100,000,000 sats (satoshis). You can start with a few dollars.
What are transaction fees?
Fees pay miners to include your transaction in a block. Higher fees = faster confirmation. Lightning offers low-fee, instant payments for daily use.
How do I keep my coins safe?
Control your private keys, use hardware wallets for savings, keep multiple backups of your seed phrase, and avoid sharing it with anyone.
Is Bitcoin legal?
Regulation varies by country. In most places it’s legal to hold, but tax and reporting rules differ—check your local guidance.
